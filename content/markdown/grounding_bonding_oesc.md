## Overview

Grounding and bonding are fundamental to electrical safety. **Grounding** establishes a reference to earth potential to stabilize system voltage and limit exposure to fault energy. **Bonding** interconnects non-current-carrying conductive parts of equipment and structures to ensure low-impedance paths that allow protective devices to operate reliably during a fault. OESC Section 10 governs both.

The overall objective (Rule 10-002) is to minimize the likelihood and severity of electric shock by establishing equipotentiality between exposed non-current-carrying conductive surfaces and nearby surfaces of the earth, and to prevent damage to property during a fault.

---

## Grounding Conductor Sizing (Rule 10-114)

The grounding conductor shall be sized not smaller than:

- **No. 6 AWG** if of copper; or
- **No. 4 AWG** if of aluminum.

The grounding conductor may be sized smaller than the above minimums provided it is not smaller than the current-carrying conductor(s) of the system being grounded.

## Bonding Conductor Sizing (Rule 10-616)

The bonding conductor is sized based on the **ampere rating or setting of the upstream overcurrent device protecting the ungrounded conductors**, or the ampacity of the largest ungrounded conductor — as determined from **Table 16**.

Key provisions:

- The bonding conductor shall not be required to be larger than the current-carrying conductors.
- Where circuit conductors are increased in size to compensate for voltage drop, the bonding conductor size shall be based on the ampacity of the largest ungrounded conductor.
- For parallel conductors in separate raceways or cables, the bonding conductor for each group shall be sized per Table 16 divided by the number of parallel groups.

## Equipotential Bonding Conductor Sizing (Rule 10-708)

For equipotential bonding conductors installed as **exposed wiring** (not subject to mechanical damage):

- **No. 6 AWG** if of copper; or
- **No. 4 AWG** if of aluminum.

For equipotential bonding conductors installed as **concealed wiring** or with mechanical protection:

- **No. 10 AWG** if of copper; or
- **No. 8 AWG** if of aluminum.

---

## Bonding Conductor Installation (Rule 10-612)

- The bonding conductor shall be permitted to be spliced or tapped.
- Where more than one bonding conductor enters a box, all such conductors shall be in electrical contact with each other.
- A bonding conductor run in the same raceway as other conductors of the circuit shall be insulated.
- Where a separate bonding conductor is run with single-conductor cables, it shall follow the same route as the cables.
- A **copper** bonding conductor of No. 6 AWG or larger shall be protected from exposure to mechanical damage where attached to a surface.
- An **aluminum** bonding conductor of No. 4 AWG or larger shall similarly be protected where exposed.

## Considerations

### Grounding vs. Bonding

It is important to distinguish between the two functions:

- **Grounding** connects the system to earth — it controls voltage to ground and provides a path for ground fault current to return to the source.
- **Bonding** connects equipment together — it ensures all metallic parts are at the same potential and provides a low-impedance fault current path to operate the overcurrent device.

Both are required; they serve different but complementary purposes.

### System Type

The grounding requirements differ depending on the system type:

- **Solidly grounded systems** — most common; a direct connection is made between the system neutral and the grounding electrode (Rules 10-200 to 10-212).
- **Impedance grounded systems** — connected to ground through an impedance device; must be maintained by a qualified person (Rule 10-300).
- **Ungrounded systems** — no intentional connection to ground; require ground fault detection (Rule 10-400).
