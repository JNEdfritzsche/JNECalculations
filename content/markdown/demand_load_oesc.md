## Overview

In any electrical installation, it is rarely the case that all connected loads operate simultaneously at their full rated capacity. The concept of a **demand load** accounts for this by applying a demand factor to the total connected load, resulting in a more realistic and economical basis for sizing services, feeders, and branch circuits. OESC Section 8 governs circuit loading and demand factors for Ontario installations.

---

## Demand Load Equation

$$
P_{demand} = P_{connected} \cdot f_{demand}
$$

**Where:**

- $P_{demand}$ = Demand load, kW
- $P_{connected}$ = Total connected load, kW
- $f_{demand}$ = Demand factor (0–1)

---

## Use of Demand Factors (Rule 8-106)

Where two or more loads are installed, demand factors may be applied so that the calculated load reflects only the maximum demand likely to occur simultaneously. Key provisions include:

- Where only one load can be used at any one time, only the one providing the greatest demand shall be used.
- Where interlocks prevent simultaneous operation of space-heating and air-conditioning, the greater load shall be used.
- Where a feeder supplies cyclic or intermittent loads such that the maximum connected load will not all be supplied at the same time, the feeder may be sized based on the maximum load that may be connected at any one time.

The demand factor is determined by the designer based on knowledge of the installation and the applicable OESC rules for the occupancy type (Rules 8-200 through 8-210).

---

## Maximum Circuit Loading (Rule 8-104)

The demand load shall not exceed the ampere rating of the circuit. The calculated load shall be considered a **continuous load** unless it can be shown that in normal operation it will not persist for:

- more than **1 hour in any 2-hour period** if the load does not exceed 225 A; or
- more than **3 hours in any 6-hour period** if the load exceeds 225 A.
